Know Your Rights with Police

üîë Most Important Thing to Remember

Always ask to speak to a lawyer. You have the right to remain silent and the right to an attorney. Use these rights!

‚ö° Quick Reference - Your Rights

  • You have the right to remain silent (except for providing your name and address when required)
  • You cannot be penalized for refusing to answer questions
  • You have the right to ask "Am I free to leave?"
  • You have the right to refuse searches (except with a warrant or probable cause)
  • If arrested, you have the right to an attorney before questioning
  • Never lie to police - but you don't have to answer their questions

If You Are Stopped by Police

üö∂‚Äç‚ôÇÔ∏è Police Approach and Question You

Police have the power to approach people and ask questions. Being approached does not mean you're suspected of a crime. Citizens are encouraged to cooperate, but with one exception (providing name and address when legally required), you have no legal duty to answer questions.

Your Rights During Police Contact:

  • You have the right of silence - you may refuse to answer questions
  • You can ask "Am I free to leave?" If yes, you can walk away
  • You should never lie to police (this can be charged as "obstructing official business")
  • Stay calm and keep your hands visible

üí° What to Say

"I am exercising my right to remain silent. I want to speak to a lawyer." Then stay quiet and don't answer questions.

‚ö†Ô∏è Important

You cannot be penalized for refusing to answer questions. Don't let anyone tell you that staying silent "makes you look guilty."

If You Are Arrested

If you are arrested, you have several important rights that police must respect:

üîç Right to Know Why You're Arrested

  • You have the right to be told why you are being arrested
  • You have the right to know the nature of the charges against you
  • Police must tell you what crime you're being arrested for

üìú Your Miranda Rights

Before being questioned following your arrest, you must be told your constitutional rights:

  • The right to remain silent and not answer any questions
  • The right to know that if you give up your right to remain silent and do answer questions, police can use your answers against you in court
  • The right to stop answering questions at any time and talk with an attorney, even if you have begun to answer questions
  • The right to speak privately with an attorney before answering any questions or signing anything
  • The right to have an attorney appointed at no cost if you cannot afford one (for crimes that carry possible jail time)

üìû Communication Rights

  • Right to contact a responsible person to tell them you've been arrested and what the charges are
  • You are not limited to one phone call - you can make more calls if needed to reach someone

üß™ Testing and Procedures

You have the right to refuse certain tests until you can talk to your lawyer:

  • Polygraph ("lie detector") tests
  • Breathalyzer or intoxilizer tests
  • Field sobriety tests (walking straight line, other physical movements)
  • Eye tests ("look-at-the-pen test")
  • Mental ability tests (reciting alphabet, doing math)

üë• Identification Procedures

  • You have the right to have your attorney present at any line-up
  • You have the right to have your attorney present during other identification procedures where possible eyewitnesses view you

üèõÔ∏è Court and Bail Rights

  • Right to reasonable bail or bond to secure your release (unless charged with a capital crime)
  • Usually a judge sets bail; for misdemeanors, police may accept bail at headquarters if no judge is available
  • Right to be brought before a court as soon as reasonably practicable after arrest
  • Right to request a preliminary hearing to test the basis of your arrest
  • Right to trial to determine guilt or innocence

Important Reminders

üö® Always Ask for a Lawyer

If you try to cooperate by answering questions while in police custody, you may create difficulties for your lawyer in defending you. Always ask to speak to a lawyer first.

ü§ù Stay Calm and Respectful

Even while exercising your rights, remain calm and respectful. Don't resist physically, even if you believe the arrest is wrong. Address legal issues in court, not on the street.

Key Points to Remember:

  • Silence is not guilt - you cannot be penalized for exercising your rights
  • Never lie - but you don't have to answer questions
  • Ask for a lawyer immediately if arrested
  • Don't sign anything without speaking to an attorney first
  • Remember details - write down what happened as soon as you can
